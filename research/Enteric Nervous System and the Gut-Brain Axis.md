    <h1>GUT-BRAIN AXIS AND NEURO-IMMUNE INTERACTION</h1>
    <p><a href="/research/">← Back to Research Overview</a></p>
  </div>

  <div class="content">
    <p>Our group focuses on the gut-brain axis and neuroimmunology, with core research targeting the regionalized coding of the enteric nervous system (ENS), neuro-innate immune crosstalk, and multiscale regulatory mechanisms of inflammation.</p>

    <p>We have developed innovative technical platforms tailored for enteric neuroscience, including RAISIN-V2-Seq and an in vivo ENS single-cell CRISPR screening system (ENS Perturb-seq), combined with STARmap PLUS spatial transcriptomics to map the gut-brain axis and dissect in situ cell-cell communication networks in homeostasis and inflammation. Using these tools, we constructed the world’s first multidimensional single-cell atlas of enteric neurons spanning multiple intestinal segments, covering varied microbiota status and inflammatory conditions, and identified key regulators governing enteric neuron cell states and their crosstalk with the microbiota and type 2 immune cells (Science, 2025; featured in FASI media report titled “Gut Neurons Decoded”).</p>

    <p>Based on our proprietary techniques and high-resolution atlases, we have revised the fundamental understanding of ENS regionalized regulation and neuro-immune interactions, providing a theoretical basis for the site-specific pathogenesis of intestinal inflammation. We identified distinct regional enrichment of enteric neuron subsets, and revealed that Nmu+ and Piezo2+ neurons mediate segment-specific type 2 inflammation via discrete receptor axes with mast cells and ILC2s. Moreover, we overturned the traditional view that adult enteric neurons are irreplaceable, by identifying adult enteric neuron progenitor cells and clarifying their microbiota- and inflammation-driven differentiation mechanisms, with Edf1 and Mitf as key regulatory molecules.</p>
